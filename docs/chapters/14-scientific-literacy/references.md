# Chapter 14: Scientific Literacy - References

1. [Scientific Method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_method) - Wikipedia - Comprehensive overview of the principles and processes of scientific inquiry, including hypothesis formation, experimentation, peer review, and the self-correcting nature of science.

2. [Scientific Consensus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_consensus) - Wikipedia - Explains how the collective judgment of the scientific community forms through accumulated evidence, peer review, and replication, with examples from climate science and medicine.

3. [List of Fallacies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_fallacies) - Wikipedia - Extensive catalog of formal and informal logical fallacies including false dichotomy, appeal to nature, cherry-picking, and correlation-causation confusion relevant to environmental debates.

4. Why Trust Science? - Naomi Oreskes - Princeton University Press - Explores why scientific consensus deserves public trust by examining the social processes of peer review, replication, and expert evaluation that make science reliable.

5. Environmental Science (16th ed.) - Miller & Spoolman - Cengage - Provides foundational coverage of scientific methodology applied to environmental research, including experimental design, statistical reasoning, and evidence evaluation.

6. [Understanding Science: How Science Really Works](https://undsci.berkeley.edu/) - UC Berkeley Museum of Paleontology - Interactive resource explaining the scientific process as a nonlinear, iterative endeavor, debunking the myth of a single rigid scientific method.

7. [SIFT Method for Evaluating Information](https://hapgood.us/2019/06/19/sift-the-four-moves/) - Mike Caulfield / Hapgood - The original description of the Stop, Investigate, Find, Trace method for rapid source evaluation, developed by a leading digital literacy researcher.

8. [Skeptical Science](https://skepticalscience.com/) - Skeptical Science - Peer-reviewed resource addressing common climate myths with evidence-based rebuttals, demonstrating how scientific literacy tools apply to real-world environmental claims.

9. [Correlation or Causation: Science-Based Medicine](https://sciencebasedmedicine.org/causation-and-correlation/) - Science-Based Medicine - Clear explanation of why correlation does not imply causation, with medical and environmental examples showing how confounding variables mislead interpretation.

10. [Statistical Literacy for Citizens](https://stattrek.com/statistics/statistical-literacy) - Stat Trek - Accessible introduction to statistical concepts including sample size, confidence intervals, margin of error, and p-values for non-specialists evaluating scientific claims.
